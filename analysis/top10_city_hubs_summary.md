## City-by-City Readout
### London
London is the broadest hub in the top-10 group once nearby metros are counted. The widened hub reaches 13 startups and functions less like a single city than a corridor linking London, Cambridge, Oxford, Reading, and Brighton. Its company mix spans hardware, software, cryptography, and materials, while its maturity profile includes research, pilot, production, and revenue-stage companies. That makes London one of the best examples in the dataset of a city hub turning into a full operating environment rather than just a startup concentration point.

### Tel Aviv
Tel Aviv is the cleanest example of a compact but high-intensity cluster. Its direct city count and hub total are both five, which means the signal is not coming from a widened corridor but from genuine metro concentration. The company set leans toward software, control systems, and commercialization layers, with Classiq, Qedma, and Quantum Machines making the city especially relevant for software-defined quantum infrastructure. Four of the five startups sit at the pilot stage, which suggests a cluster already oriented toward productization rather than purely academic research.

### Cambridge (UK)
Cambridge behaves like a second entry point into the same broader UK corridor, but it still deserves separate treatment because of its distinct company mix and density. The hub reaches 10 startups and includes Quantinuum, Riverlane, Nu Quantum, and Cambridge Quantum Computing, along with adjacent London-based software and cryptography companies. The result is a balanced cluster with strong hardware-software co-presence and one of the clearest concentrations of full-stack and infrastructure-layer quantum companies in the dataset.

### Boston
Boston is smaller than the UK corridors, but its mix is strategically important. The hub reaches four startups and combines neutral-atom hardware with enterprise-facing software. QuEra Computing, QuEra, Zapata Computing, and Aliro Quantum give Boston a profile that blends core hardware development with workflow and access layers. The maturity mix is still pilot-heavy, which suggests a cluster moving toward commercialization without yet reaching the breadth of London or Cambridge.

### Bristol
Bristol is a highly specialized hub rather than a broad one. Its three-company footprint includes Phasecraft, Qlm Technology Ltd, and KETS Quantum Security. That gives the city an unusual mix of software, materials, sensing, and cryptography. Bristol is not large enough to dominate on scale, but it stands out for its technical specificity and its role as a niche node in the wider UK quantum landscape.

### Kansas
Kansas is one of the more surprising entries in the top-10 list because it is small but unusually balanced. QC Ware Corp., ColdQuanta Inc, and Quantum Xchange create a three-company hub that spans software, sensing, hardware, and cryptography without one specialty clearly dominating. Its maturity mix leans pilot, but with full funding disclosure across all three companies. That gives Kansas a distinctive profile: not a dense corridor, but a compact multipolar hub with a broad set of quantum-adjacent capabilities.

### Paris
Paris is one of the most technically concentrated hubs in the dataset. The widened hub reaches four startups: Pasqal, Quandela, Alice & Bob, and C12 Quantum Electronics. Together they cover neutral atoms, photonics, fault-tolerant ambition, and materials-heavy hardware. Paris is more hardware-led than some of the software-rich hubs, but its combination of architectures makes it one of the most strategically interesting cities for insiders watching core compute development.

### San Francisco Bay
San Francisco Bay is another case where the city count understates the real regional signal. The hub expands from a direct city count of three to a corridor total of seven once Palo Alto, Berkeley, and Menlo Park are included. That widened picture captures Rigetti Computing, SandboxAQ, PsiQuantum, QuSecure, Brilliant, Menten AI, and Sense Photonics. The Bay's profile is especially notable for its software, cryptography, and materials presence, combined with large disclosed funding totals and at least one revenue-stage company. It reads less like a single-city cluster and more like an integrated capital-and-platform corridor.

### Singapore
Singapore is compact, cleanly defined, and strongly tilted toward software and communications. The hub contains Horizon Quantum, Horizon Quantum Computing, and SpeQtral. The result is a small but legible cluster that mixes software-toolchain development with quantum communications. Like Tel Aviv, Singapore benefits from being easy to describe: the cluster is small, concentrated, and commercially oriented, even if it does not yet match the scale of the larger corridors.

### Sydney
Sydney rounds out the top-10 list with a research-heavy but strategically important mix. Q-CTRL, Diraq, and Silicon Quantum Computing give the city a profile anchored in control software, silicon hardware, and deep research depth. Two of the three companies are still classified as research-stage, which makes Sydney less commercially mature than Tel Aviv or London, but potentially strong as a future hardware-and-control innovation center.

## What the Top 10 Shows
Three broad patterns emerge from the top-10 city-hub view. First, the most powerful quantum hubs are not always single cities. London, Cambridge, and San Francisco grow materially when widened into nearby corridors, which suggests that adjacency matters as much as municipal boundaries. Second, software and hardware repeatedly co-exist in the strongest hubs, reinforcing the broader finding that the ecosystem is moving toward full-stack and software-defined models. Third, maturity is uneven. Some hubs are still research-heavy, while others already show pilot, production, or revenue-stage signals. That matters because city hubs should not be judged only by startup count; they should also be judged by how effectively they translate technical density into commercialization.
