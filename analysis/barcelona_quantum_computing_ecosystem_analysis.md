# The State of Quantum Computing in Barcelona

As of April 27, 2026, Barcelona is not one of the world's largest quantum computing startup hubs by company count. It is, however, one of the more coherent quantum ecosystems in Europe. Its importance comes less from scale than from structure. Barcelona has a small but clearly organized cluster built around research institutions, photonics expertise, supercomputing infrastructure, and a handful of startups that occupy different layers of the quantum stack.

The key to understanding Barcelona is that its quantum economy is broader than quantum computing alone. The city's real strength is a corridor that links Barcelona and nearby Castelldefels through ICFO, the Barcelona Supercomputing Center (BSC), IFAE, and the regional university system. In this corridor, computing, communications, cybersecurity, photonics, and talent formation reinforce one another.

The leading pure-play quantum computing company is **Qilimanjaro Quantum Tech**. That matters because Barcelona does not yet have a large field of competing compute startups pursuing many different qubit modalities. Instead, it has one flagship company trying to establish itself as the local systems integrator and hardware champion. Qilimanjaro describes itself as a builder of analog, full-stack quantum computers spanning theory, applications, software, and hardware. That is an ambitious position, but it is also a logical one in Barcelona, where the ecosystem rewards integration more than fragmentation.

Qilimanjaro's strategic importance has grown because it is connected to real infrastructure rather than only to research narratives. Its public materials highlight deployment at BSC and broader work around multimodal quantum data center infrastructure. The January 2025 announcements around **MareNostrum-Ona** pushed that point further. Qilimanjaro is not just a startup located in Barcelona; it is part of Barcelona's attempt to build a hybrid HPC-plus-quantum capability with local industrial ownership.

This institutional linkage is one of Barcelona's biggest advantages. BSC contributes quantum algorithms, quantum-HPC integration, and national coordination through **Quantum Spain**. IFAE contributes superconducting-circuit capability and laboratory depth through its Quantum Computing Technology group. The University of Barcelona and nearby universities support the scientific pipeline and feed talent into the system. In Barcelona, the ties between startups and research infrastructure are tighter and more operational than in many other cities.

If Qilimanjaro is the core compute player, **Quside** and **LuxQuanta** show where Barcelona may already be more commercially grounded than some larger quantum hubs: secure communications and quantum-safe infrastructure.

Quside, an ICFO spin-off launched in 2017, develops quantum randomness products for cybersecurity and high-performance computing. This is one of the most practical areas of quantum commercialization because it does not depend on waiting for fault-tolerant quantum computers. By 2023 the company was publicly describing itself as having moved into commercialization mode, and it has identified customers and partners such as Telefonica, Juniper, Qrypt, and EY. In the Barcelona ecosystem, Quside represents a quantum company selling into infrastructure markets now, not only into a future computing market.

LuxQuanta occupies a neighboring position. Also an ICFO spin-off, founded in 2021, it focuses on quantum key distribution for telecom networks. That makes it part of Europe's push to build quantum-safe communications rather than part of the race to build universal quantum processors. LuxQuanta's launch of a second-generation QKD system at Mobile World Congress 2025 was a meaningful signal for Barcelona. It showed that the city is not only producing research programs in quantum networking, but also product companies trying to integrate with existing fiber and telecom environments. LuxQuanta therefore extends Barcelona's role from computing ambition into communications deployment.

Around these three companies sits a second layer of enabling and adjacent deep-tech firms. **Qurv Technologies** is better understood as a quantum-adjacent imaging and advanced sensor company than as a direct compute player. **shinephi**, launched in 2025 and recognized by ACCIO as Catalan Startup of the Year 2025, is a photonics and semiconductor metrology company. **MAPSI Photonics** contributes component and photonics depth. These firms matter because successful quantum regions also need tools, components, measurement systems, and adjacent optical capabilities that improve the industrial base.

That is why Barcelona is best described as a **quantum-and-photonics ecosystem** rather than as a standalone quantum computing capital. ICFO gives Barcelona a globally credible center of gravity in photonics and quantum technologies. BSC gives it a bridge into high-performance computing and national infrastructure. Catalonia's 2025 **Quantum Valley** initiative, coordinated scientifically by ICFO, signals that the region sees quantum as an ecosystem-building exercise involving research, company creation, talent, and industrial application. The interuniversity **Master in Quantum Science and Technology** across UB, UAB, and UPC reinforces that pipeline.

The main weakness is scale. Barcelona still lacks the breadth of startup formation seen in the strongest global hubs. It does not yet have a thick layer of independent software firms, control-stack specialists, cryogenic suppliers, and multiple competing compute platforms. The cluster is concentrated around a few institutional sources, above all ICFO and the BSC-IFAE-UB orbit. If Barcelona wants to become a first-rank quantum computing city rather than a promising specialized node, it will need a second generation of companies beyond the first wave of spin-offs.

Even so, the current position is stronger than it may first appear. Barcelona already has a flagship compute company in Qilimanjaro, commercially relevant communications and security players in Quside and LuxQuanta, a useful bench of enabling photonics firms, and one of Europe's better institutional setups for translating quantum science into companies. The ecosystem is still early and still smaller than the largest hubs. But it is no longer speculative. Barcelona's quantum identity is becoming real through a clear division of labor: computing at the core, quantum-safe communications close behind, and photonics as the industrial substrate that helps the whole system hold together.
